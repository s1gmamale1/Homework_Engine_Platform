## 1. Purpose

Real-Life Challenge is the Phase 4 transfer mechanic. The student must apply the current lesson to a realistic Uzbek-context case and explain the decision.

This mechanic exists to test transfer, not recall. It sits between practice and Final Boss:
- Story Mode teaches
- Game Breaks rehearse
- Real-Life Challenge transfers
- Final Boss certifies

---

## 2. Non-Negotiable Rules

1. The student must be addressed in first-person expert POV: `You are...`, `Your job is...`
2. The student must be the expert, not an observer.
3. The case must be solvable using current-lesson knowledge only.
4. Explanation is required. The student must answer both `what` and `why`.
5. Wrong options must be plausible enough to expose weak reasoning.
6. The setting should feel believable and may use local or broader industry applications.
7. Students below PISA Level 2 do not get this mechanic. They get extra Apply-level practice instead.

---

## 3. Standard Shape

| Item | Standard Mode | Extended Mode |
|---|---|---|
| Duration | 3-5 min | 10 min |
| Scenario count | 1 | 1 |
| Sub-questions | 2-3 | 3-5 |
| Minimum level | PISA L2+ | PISA L2+ |
| Output | decision + explanation | decision + explanation + comparison |

Recommended question flow:
1. Understand the case
2. Apply the lesson concept
3. Evaluate in context

Extended mode can add:
4. Compare alternatives
5. Give a final recommendation

---

## 4. Core Structure

Every Real-Life Challenge should contain:

### Expert Frame
Examples:
- `You are a fire safety inspector...`
- `You are a transport pricing advisor...`
- `You are a museum fact-checker...`

### Situation Brief
A short case with concrete constraints, numbers, and a real decision.

### Evidence Set
Everything needed to solve the task:
- table
- short text
- diagram
- map
- timeline

No hidden knowledge.

### Decision Prompt
Use verbs like:
- choose
- compare
- recommend
- diagnose
- prioritize
- explain

---

## 5. Scoring

| Dimension | Weight |
|---|---:|
| Decision accuracy | 40% |
| Reasoning quality | 60% |

Reasoning is weighted higher because transfer is the purpose of the mechanic.

Simple rubric:
- `Off-track`: wrong concept or ignores case facts
- `Partial`: some correct work, weak explanation
- `Solid`: correct conclusion with mostly correct reasoning
- `Strong transfer`: correct conclusion, evidence-based explanation, handles constraints well

---

## 6. Best Variations

### Choose the Best Option
Compare 2-3 options and recommend one.

### Diagnose the Problem
Find what is wrong and explain why.

### Prioritize the First Move
Choose the best first action under constraints.

### Compare Claims
Decide which claim is stronger and justify it.

## 8. Anti-Patterns

Reject the scenario if it is:
- third-person spectator framing
- a normal worksheet with fake real-life names pasted on top
- dependent on outside knowledge
- a single-step textbook question disguised as transfer
- explanation-free

---

## 9. Acceptance Checklist

A scenario is ready only if:
- the student is explicitly the expert
- the task requires current-lesson transfer, not recall only
- all required facts are supplied
- explanation is mandatory
- wrong paths are plausible
- the context feels believable and appropriately applied
- the full task fits Phase 4 time limits

---

## 10. Canonical Example

**Bad:**  
`A taxi costs 2000 + 1500 per km. What is the price for 5 km?`

**Good:**  
`You are advising the city council on taxi pricing. Company A charges 2000 UZS base + 1500 per km. Company B charges 3000 UZS base + 1000 per km. At what distance do they cost the same? A commuter says Company B is always cheaper. Is that true? Write your recommendation and explain why.`

That is the standard: expert role, real options, applied concept, and explanation.
