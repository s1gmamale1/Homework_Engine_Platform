## 1. What English Teaches

English in NETS is a **foreign language**, not a native or heritage language. That changes the production rules.

- Students often start from near-zero or highly uneven exposure.
- Krashen's **i+1** calibration matters more than in Uzbek or Russian because the comprehension gap can break the session if input jumps too far.
- **CEFR progression** is the spine of the subject: **A1 (G5) -> A2 (G6-7) -> B1 (G8-9) -> B2 (G10-11)**.
- The textbook is the primary input source because many students do not meet English naturally outside class.
- Listening is usually harder than reading because environmental English exposure is weak.
- Production lags comprehension by 1-2 levels. This is expected and must not be treated as failure.
- The agent's job is to **transform textbook topics into strong homework**, not to copy weak textbook pedagogy. If the explanation is dry, Story Mode makes it clearer. If the exercise set is boring, Game Breaks and Real-Life Challenge make it active and engaging without changing the textbook topic.

**What makes English different from other Til Fanlari subjects:**
- **Uzbek:** student already speaks it; homework formalizes grammar they partly know intuitively.
- **Russian:** many students have partial exposure; homework bridges gaps.
- **English:** homework itself is a major learning engine, so scaffolding, repetition, and pacing must be tighter.

**Core production rule:** For English, assume less background knowledge, more support need, and slower release from scaffolding than for other language subjects.

## 2. Grade-Band Progression

| Grade | CEFR Target | Focus | Production Level | Boss Expectations |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **G5** | **A1** | Vocabulary building, basic sentence patterns, phonics awareness | Guided: sentence stems, word banks | Fill-in + guided 1-2 sentence answers |
| **G6** | **A1->A2** | Simple conversations, present/past tense, reading short passages | Guided -> Prompted: complete sentences with prompts | 3-4 sentence guided responses |
| **G7** | **A2** | Paragraph reading, question formation, basic composition | Prompted: write 3-5 sentences on a topic | Short paragraph with structure cues |
| **G8** | **A2->B1** | Complex sentences, inferencing, summary writing | Prompted -> Free: paragraph without stems | Full paragraph, must show reasoning |
| **G9** | **B1** | Essay structure intro, formal/informal register, listening comprehension | Free: introduction + body + conclusion | 5-8 sentence structured response |
| **G10** | **B1->B2** | Argumentation, text analysis, advanced grammar | Free: multi-paragraph with thesis | Analytical response with evidence |
| **G11** | **B2** | Advanced textbook grammar, composition, text analysis, advanced listening | Independent: essay-level production | Extended composition, academic register |

**Reference anchors:** [[UNIFIED-Buzan §19]], [[NETS-Library-Framework.md]] Til Fanlari levels and paths.

## 3. Content Production Rules by Grade Band

### G5-6 (Foundation)

- Maximum **10 new vocabulary items** per session.
- Every new word gets: **image + Uzbek translation + audio pronunciation + example sentence**.
- Grammar is taught through patterns in context, never as isolated rule-first explanation.
- Reading passages: **50-80 words max**.
- Listening clips: **slow, clear speech, max 30 seconds**.
- Phase 2 Story Mode: **bilingual scaffolding is allowed**. Uzbek setup, English core content.
- Phase 6 Boss: guided production with stems.
- If the textbook is dry, make it alive: use Story Mode to add narrative and use Real-Life Challenge to add a concrete communication purpose the textbook does not provide.

**Good production pattern:**  
"My favorite animal is ___. It lives in ___. It eats ___."

**Good Phase 4 example:**  
"You are a tour guide at Registan Square. A tourist asks, 'What is this place?' Answer in 2 sentences using: old, beautiful, Samarkand."

**Bad Phase 4 example:**  
"Write an essay about tourism in Uzbekistan."

### G7-9 (Development)

- Target **10+ new vocabulary items** per session.
- Grammar can include: past continuous, conditionals (1st/2nd), passive voice, relative clauses.
- Reading passages: **100-200 words**.
- Listening: **natural speed**, usually **45-60 seconds**. Limited background noise can appear by G9.
- Phase 2 Story Mode: **English-only narrative** with Uzbek glossary support for hard words.
- Phase 6 Boss: prompted free production.
- Why Chain may extend to **3 levels** for grammar and discourse reasoning.
- If the textbook is dry, make it alive: build a narrative around the target grammar or reading passage, and create role-based communication tasks instead of leaving the student with flat rule drills.

**Good production pattern:**  
"Write 5 sentences comparing city life and village life. Use at least 2 comparatives."

**Good Phase 4 example:**  
"You are a hotel receptionist. A guest complains about the room. Respond politely and offer a solution."

**Bad Phase 4 example:**  
"Explain all English tenses with examples."

### G10-11 (Advanced)

- Target **15+ vocabulary items** when the chapter supports it, including academic word sets.
- Grammar may cover all tense systems, reported speech, advanced conditionals, formal register, and complex clause structures.
- Reading passages: **300-500 words** for standard advanced work.
- Listening: **2-3 minutes**, authentic materials, multiple speakers when the source supports it.
- Phase 2 Story Mode: **fully English**, no Uzbek scaffolding in the main content.
- Phase 6 Boss: extended composition with thesis, support, and conclusion.
- If the textbook is dry, make it alive: Story Mode should create interpretive tension and Real-Life Challenge should create professional or civic context that the raw textbook page often lacks.

**Good production pattern:**  
"Some people believe technology makes students lazy. To what extent do you agree? Write 150-200 words with introduction, body paragraphs, and conclusion."

**Good Phase 4 example:**  
"You are an environmental consultant. Write a report recommending 3 actions to reduce plastic waste in Tashkent. Support each with evidence."

**Bad Phase 4 example:**  
"Memorize 20 advanced words and write their dictionary definitions."

## 4. Game Mechanics — English-Specific Filtering

English inherits the Til Fanlari mechanic set, then narrows it further.

**Always-primary for English:**
- **Spaced Flashcards** — vocabulary retention is the main bottleneck.
- **Sentence Fill / Cloze** — grammar must stay in context.
- **Listening Tasks** — should appear in at least **2 out of 3 sessions** unless the current textbook unit is explicitly non-audio.

### Primary Mechanics by Grade Band

| Mechanic | G5-6 | G7-9 | G10-11 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Spaced Flashcards** | 10 words, image + UZ translation | 10+ words, English definition + example | 15+ words, academic set, English-only |
| **Sentence Fill** | Word bank provided, 1 blank per sentence | 2-3 blanks, no word bank by G9 | Full cloze paragraph, no aids |
| **Listening** | 30 sec, slow, clear | 60 sec, natural speed | 2-3 min, multiple speakers |
| **Dialogue** | Scripted response, choose/complete | Prompted response, student writes own answer | Free response, minimal scaffolding |
| **Dictation** | Single sentences, slow delivery | Short paragraph, natural speed | Academic paragraph, first listen = notes only |
| **Story Mode** | Bilingual scaffolding OK | English-only + glossary | Fully English, literary or authentic texts |

### Interactive Picks for English

| Game | Best Use |
|---|---|
| **Memory Match Blitz** | G5-7 word-translation pairs; G8-11 synonym-antonym or collocation pairs |
| **Codebreaker** | Spelling patterns, decoding, affixes, pronunciation-linked orthography |
| **Reaction Chain** | Sentence construction or dialogue sequence, best from G7+ |
| **Puzzle Lock** | Paragraph reconstruction, text cohesion, best from G9+ |

### Mechanics That Work Differently in English

#### **Tile Match**
- Use for **word order and sentence assembly**, not formula assembly.
- **Good Example:** "Drag words into correct order: `[went / to / I / school / yesterday]`."
- **Bad Example:** Matching grammar labels to definitions with no sentence context.

#### **Word Ladder**
- Use for **morphological awareness** and word families.
- **Good Example:** `run -> ran -> running -> runner`.
- **Bad Example:** Random rhyming words with no grammar or vocabulary target.

### Games to Skip for English

- **Notebook Capture** — English does not need derivation-style handwritten submission as a core mechanic.
- **Error Analysis (math-style)** — use proofreading, editing, and sentence repair inside Cloze or writing tasks instead.
- **Speed Match** — arithmetic-oriented and misaligned with language production goals.

**Dual-Catalog inheritance:** Phase 3 still requires at least **1 Interactive** and **2 Default** mechanics (see [[UNIFIED-Buzan §5.3]]).

## 5. Boss Format — English Specific

English inherits the Til Fanlari Boss model: writing production, rubric-based evaluation, TEFCAS framing, and universal HP/stars rules.

| Grade | Boss Type | Format | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **G5** | Guided production | Sentence stems + word bank | "Complete: My school is ___. I like ___ because ___." |
| **G6** | Guided -> Prompted | 3-4 sentences with topic + required words | "Write 3 sentences about your family. Use: live, work, favorite." |
| **G7** | Prompted | 5 sentences on topic with grammar requirement | "Describe your last holiday. Use past tense. At least 5 sentences." |
| **G8** | Prompted -> Free | Paragraph with structure hints | "Write a paragraph about pollution. Include: cause, effect, solution." |
| **G9** | Free production | Full paragraph graded on structure + grammar | "Do you think homework is important? Explain your opinion in one paragraph." |
| **G10** | Essay intro | 2 paragraphs with thesis | "Some people prefer cities. Others prefer villages. Compare both and state your preference." |
| **G11** | Extended composition | 150-200 word composition, intro + body + conclusion | "Technology has changed the way we learn. Explain the advantages and disadvantages, then state your view." |

**All-grade Boss rules:**
- **HP:** G5-8 = 100, G9-11 = 150 ([[UNIFIED-Buzan §5.6]]).
- **G5 exception:** Up to 30% MC allowed where needed.
- **Damage and stars:** universal Boss rules apply unchanged.
- **Hint cost:** -10 HP.
- **Failure framing:** use TEFCAS language, e.g. **"Hali emas! Let's try again."**

**Good Boss design:** asks the student to produce meaning in context, with grammar and structure serving communication.

**Bad Boss design:** asks for isolated rule recall, e.g. "What is the definition of the present perfect tense?"

## 6. Story Mode & Real-Life Challenge — English Adaptations

### Story Mode by Grade

| Grade | Scaffolding | Narrative Style | Checkpoint Type |
|---|---|---|---|
| **G5-6** | Bilingual (Uzbek intro -> English content) | Simple story with repetition, graded-reader style | Vocabulary match, basic comprehension |
| **G7-8** | English-only + glossary sidebar | Adventure, travel, school, or mystery narrative | Inference, grammar-in-context |
| **G9-11** | English-only, no aids in core text | Literary excerpt or authentic text | Analysis, interpretation, short writing response |

**Good Story Mode (G7):**  
"Alex is visiting Bukhara. He needs to ask for directions to the Ark Fortress. The locals speak some English. Follow his journey and help him communicate."

**Bad Story Mode:**  
"Today we learn prepositions. In, on, at, under, behind. Read these 10 sentences."

### Real-Life Challenge by Grade

| Grade | Role | Scenario Complexity |
|---|---|---|
| **G5-6** | Simple helper | "You meet a tourist. They're lost. Help them find the bazaar using: go, turn, near." |
| **G7-8** | Service professional | "You work at a hotel. A guest emails asking about room prices. Write a reply." |
| **G9-10** | Expert communicator | "You are a journalist interviewing a scientist about climate change. Write 5 interview questions." |
| **G11** | Academic/professional | "You are applying for an IT Park internship. Write a cover letter explaining your skills." |

**All Real-Life Challenge rules for English:**
- First-person or direct role POV is mandatory.
- Uzbek cultural context is preferred unless the chapter naturally calls for a global setting.
- National Pride balance is inherited from parent docs; do not redefine ratios here.

**Good G5-6 example:** practical, concrete, short, role-based.

**Bad G5-6 example:** abstract policy essay or multi-paragraph persuasion task.

**Good G10-11 example:** professional communication with evidence, purpose, and audience.

**Bad G10-11 example:** disconnected grammar worksheet disguised as a scenario.

## 7. Curriculum Touchpoints

- The **textbook chapter remains the content source**. Homework must track the chapter the student studied in class.
- English has a special production risk: **textbook quality and depth may vary by grade**, so agents must adapt to the actual chapter rather than assume a perfectly standardized ladder.
- In this workspace, the **English textbook directories for G5-11 exist but are currently empty**, so this framework relies on the parent standards and CEFR/library progression rather than local TOC extraction.
