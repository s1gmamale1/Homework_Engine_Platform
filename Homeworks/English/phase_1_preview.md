## Panel 2: Better Explanation
When we talk about rules or things that are necessary, we use a special grammar formula. The formula is `Subject + have to / has to + base verb`. If something is not necessary, we use `Subject + don't have to / doesn't have to + base verb`. In Uzbek, "have to" maps to "Siz buni bajarishingiz shart", showing that you have no choice. On the other hand, "don't have to" maps to "Siz buni bajarishingiz shart emas", which means you can do it if you want, but it is not an obligation. For example, if you "don't have to" wear a uniform, you can wear your own clothes. A common mistake is thinking that "don't have to" means you must not do something. Remember, "don't have to" gives you freedom and a choice. Stress is also important when speaking; for example, in the word **aquarium**, the stress falls on the second syllable /əˈkweə.ri.əm/. This mental model will help you understand rules in any English-speaking environment.

*(Qisqacha mazmuni: Qoidalarni tushuntirish uchun `have to` (bajarish shart) va `don't have to` (bajarish shart emas) qurilmalaridan foydalanamiz. "Don't have to" sizga biror ishni qilish yoki qilmaslik erkinligini beradi — uni "qilish taqiqlanadi" deb adashmang.)*
[Bloom: L2 | PISA: L2]

## Panel 3: Examples
Let's look at some real sentences from your textbook to see this in action. On page 113, the book says, "Everyone **has to** be at school at eight o'clock." This shows a clear rule that all students must follow without a choice. Another example from page 113 is, "You **don't have to** bring anything to eat." This means bringing food is not an obligation, but you can bring it if you are hungry. Finally, on page 113, we see, "My dad **doesn't have to** wear a suit at work." This explains that his workplace gives him the freedom to wear comfortable clothes. Notice how the verb after "have to" or "don't have to" is always in the base form. Be careful with the false friend "gymnasium"; in English, a gym is a place for sports, not an academic school. By studying these examples, you can see exactly how the rules work in daily life.

## Panel 5: Word → Structure Translation
This is where you learn to extract the pattern from real situations and produce your own sentences. First, imagine you read a news article: "The airline announced that all passengers **have to** show their passports." The target here is `have to + base verb`, which maps to "Siz ko'rsatishingiz shart" in Uzbek. We extract the core structure: `subject + have to + base verb`. Now you can produce your own version: "Every student **has to** pass the final test." Next, you see a sign at a museum: "Children under twelve **don't have to** pay for tickets." The target is `don't have to + base verb`, mapping to "Siz to'lashingiz shart emas". We extract the structure `subject + don't have to + base verb`, and produce: "We **don't have to** wear a uniform on the school trip." Finally, you hear a manager say: "The new receptionist **has to** speak English and Russian." Here the target is `has to + base verb` for the third person, mapping to "U gapirishi shart", so we produce: "My brother **has to** do his homework before dinner." By practising this translation method, you can easily change the words to fit your own life.

*(Qisqacha mazmuni: Bu yerda biz inglizcha jumlalardagi qolipni ajratib olib, o'z gaplarimizni tuzishni o'rganamiz. Masalan, "yo'lovchilar pasport ko'rsatishi shart" gapi orqali `subject + have to + base verb` qolipini bilib olib, uni "o'quvchilar imtihondan o'tishi shart" degan o'z gapimizga aylantiramiz.)*

## Panel 7: Mental Model
To remember this lesson, you can use a simple visual analogy in your mind. Think of "have to" as a locked door that you must unlock to go forward. You have no other choice but to open that specific door. Now, think of "don't have to" as an open gate in a beautiful garden. You can walk through the gate if you want, but you can also choose to walk around it. In Uzbek, the locked door is "Siz buni bajarishingiz shart" (obligation), and the open gate is "Siz buni bajarishingiz shart emas" (optional). Whenever you are not sure which phrase to use, picture the door and the gate.
