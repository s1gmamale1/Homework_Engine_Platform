1. Core Game Mechanics

Escape Room: Subject Lock presents the student with 4 locked objects (doors, chests, devices, vaults) arranged in a room or chamber. Each object has a lock requiring a correct answer to open. The defining innovation is the cross-hinting structure: clues for one lock are partially hidden within the answers and feedback of other locks.

  • Setup: The room contains 4 locked objects, each labeled with a cryptic title (e.g., "The Iron Door," "The Cipher Chest," "The Clockwork Device," "The Obsidian Vault"). Each lock has a visible keyhole showing a hint fragment — a partial word, number, symbol, or image.
  • Unlock Mechanism: The student selects a locked object and receives a subject question. Correct answer = the object unlocks and reveals its contents plus a clue fragment that helps with one or more other locked objects. Wrong answer = the object remains locked, the student receives a "lockout penalty" (one hint fragment is permanently erased from their clue list), and they may retry the same object or switch to another.
  • Cross-Hinting: Solving one lock reveals clue fragments for other locks. For example, unlocking "The Iron Door" (a math question) might reveal: "The third digit of the Cipher Chest's code is the same as the answer to the Iron Door." This forces the student to integrate knowledge across locks — solving one genuinely helps with others.
  • Cross-Subject Review: In Big Boss sessions, the 4 locks draw from different subjects. A math lock's clue might reveal a fact needed for a science lock. A history lock's answer might contain a number needed for a math lock. This creates forced cross-domain synthesis — the student cannot silo their thinking.

Lock difficulty scales by grade:

  • Grades 1-2: 4 simple locks with single-word/number answers. Clues are visual (matching shapes, colors). 15:00 timer.
  • Grades 3-4: 4 locks with short-answer or multiple-choice questions. Clues are word fragments or numbers. 12:00 timer.
  • Grades 5-7: 4 locks with multi-step reasoning. Clues include partial formulas, coded phrases. 10:00 timer.
  • Grades 8-11: 4 locks with complex synthesis questions. Clues require interpretation (e.g., "The answer to Lock 2 modulo 3 gives the first digit of Lock 4's code"). 8:00 timer.

Retry Rule: Each lock can be attempted unlimited times, but each wrong attempt erases one hint fragment from the student's clue board. After 3 wrong attempts on the same lock, the lock "degrades" — the question difficulty increases by one PISA level (the lock is learning the student's weakness).

Hint System

The student starts with 2 free hint fragments visible on their clue board. Additional hint fragments are earned by unlocking other locks. The hint system works as follows:

  • Direct Clue: "The answer to Lock 1 is a prime number. Lock 3's code is that number + 5." This directly links two locks.
  • Elimination Clue: "Lock 2's answer does NOT contain the letter 'E'." This narrows possibilities without giving the answer.
  • Cross-Subject Clue: "The year of the Battle of Anantar (History, Lock 4) minus 500 equals the answer to Lock 1 (Math)." This forces the student to solve the history question to unlock the math question.
  • Meta Clue (rare, ~10% of rooms): "All four answers share a common property." The student must identify the property (e.g., all are even numbers, all contain the letter 'A', all are divisible by 7) to narrow their search space.

🧠 Buzan Integration: Associative Lock Discovery

Escape Room: Subject Lock implements Tony Buzan's associative thinking and color-coded imagery principles to transform isolated facts into interconnected knowledge networks.

  • Color Hooks (Lock Identity): Each of the 4 locks is assigned a distinct color. When a lock opens, its color floods across the clue board (0.5s pulse), and all clue fragments related to that lock glow in that color. This creates a vivid visual map of which locks have been solved and which remain. Unsolved locks pulse dimly in their color, creating a "calling" effect that draws attention to remaining challenges.
  • Radiant Branches (Clue Web): When the student unlocks their first lock, the clue fragment for the second lock appears as a "branch" extending from the solved lock. Each subsequent unlock extends the branch further. By the time all 4 locks are open, the clue board displays a radiant web connecting all four — a visual representation of how knowledge domains interconnect. This web is saved to the student's match history gallery.
  • Imagery & Exaggeration (Lock Personalities): Each lock has an exaggerated visual personality. "The Iron Door" is massive, rusted, with a keyhole shaped like a math symbol. "The Cipher Chest" is ornate, covered in coded symbols, with a combination dial that spins on its own. "The Clockwork Device" ticks audibly, its gears visible through glass. "The Obsidian Vault" absorbs light, its surface reflecting distorted images of the student's past answers. These exaggerated features create strong episodic memories.
  • Memory Palace Anchor (Room as Palace): The escape room itself functions as a mini Memory Palace. Each locked object occupies a distinct "room location." When all 4 locks are opened, the room transforms into a bright, open space — the "palace revealed." The student can revisit this room in their gallery, and the spatial layout of the 4 objects serves as anchor points for the knowledge they unlocked. Students who complete 10+ Escape Rooms unlock the "Master Escapist" achievement in the Bilim Bazasi.

Each lock presents a question scaled to the target PISA level. The cross-hinting structure means questions are never isolated — answers to one lock provide genuine utility for solving others.

C. Escape Outcome Grades

Full Escape Victory (all 4 locks unlocked):

  • Perfect Escape (0 wrong attempts): Mastered-level grade. "Flawless Escapist" badge. The room transforms into a sunlit garden celebration.
  • Clean Escape (1-2 wrong): Proficient-level grade. The room opens partially — one wall dissolves to reveal the outside.
  • Hard Escape (3+ wrong): Apprentice-level grade. The room's exit creaks open slowly — earned through persistence.

Timer Exhaustion (Partial Escape):

  • 3 of 4 unlocked: "Hali emas" screen. The remaining lock is shown with its correct answer and the clue that would have helped. Partial accuracy credit for 3 locks. No forced replay — counted as a near-miss learning session.
  • 2 or fewer unlocked: "Hali emas" screen. All remaining locks are revealed with correct answers shown for 10 seconds. Student earns accuracy credit only for unlocked locks. The room is marked as "pending mastery" and will reappear in a future session with the same lock configuration but fresh questions.

D. Defeat Framing

Failure is framed as "The room still holds its secrets" — never as personal inadequacy. The "Hali emas" screen shows the locked objects with a dim glow, as if waiting for the student's return. All correct answers are revealed for study. The message reads: "The locks were strong, but your mind is stronger. Study these clues and try again." The room persists in the student's pending list, guaranteeing a rematch with the same structural puzzle (different questions) in a future session.
